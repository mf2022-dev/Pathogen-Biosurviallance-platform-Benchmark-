1. Executive Summary

This report presents the findings of the most comprehensive evaluation of global pathogen surveillance platforms to date. Using the Pathogen Surveillance Evaluation Framework (PSEF) v3.0, we assessed 169 platforms across 10 weighted dimensions, generating 1,690 individual data points that form the basis for capability gap identification, investment prioritization, and strategic recommendations.

Bottom line: The global pathogen surveillance landscape is fragmented, with significant capability concentrated in a small number of elite platforms. Only 1.8% of evaluated platforms achieve Excellent status (90+), while 8.9% remain in the Developing tier (<70). Critical gaps persist in genomic sequencing equity, AI/ML integration, interoperability, and One Health convergence.

Key Takeaways

  1. Extreme concentration of capability — The top 10 platforms (5.9%) account for a disproportionate share of global surveillance capacity, with 80% of genomic sequencing concentrated in Five Eyes + EU.
  2. Security is the strongest dimension (82.5 avg), while accessibility is the weakest (73.9 avg) — many platforms remain inaccessible to LMICs.
  3. Adversary capabilities are accelerating — China's AI-integrated biosurveillance and Russia's VECTOR modernization represent growing dual-use concerns.
  4. $31.6 billion in linked investment has been mapped to platform capabilities across 8 regions, with 96 policy recommendations generated.
  5. Interoperability remains a systemic weakness (77.3 avg) — HL7/FHIR adoption is inconsistent, and defense platforms operate in classified silos.

2.3 Layer Classification

Platforms are organized into five capability layers representing the bio-intelligence stack:

  • L1 — Surveillance (63 platforms): Disease monitoring, syndromic surveillance, epidemic intelligence, event-based monitoring
  • L2 — Genomic (57 platforms): Phylogenetic analysis, sequencing pipelines, bioinformatics, variant tracking
  • L3 — Defense (35 platforms): Military biodefense, CBRN programs, bioweapons assessment, countermeasure R&D
  • L4 — Hardware (9 platforms): Biological point detection, aerosol collection, field diagnostic devices
  • L5 — Policy (5 platforms): Health security assessment, IHR compliance, pandemic preparedness frameworks

3. Tier Analysis

The distribution of platforms across tiers reveals a landscape where the bulk of platforms (≈89%) cluster in the Good and Adequate tiers, with excellence being rare and developing-status platforms concentrated in LMICs and classified programs with limited transparency.

3.1 Excellent Tier (Score ≥90)

Only three platforms achieved Excellent status, representing the gold standard in pathogen surveillance:

  • Nextstrain (95) — Open-source phylogenetic analysis platform from Fred Hutchinson Cancer Center, providing real-time tracking of pathogen evolution with unmatched visualization.
  • SORMAS (90) — End-to-end outbreak management system deployed in 15+ countries, with mobile capabilities and WHO endorsement.
  • outbreak.info (90) — Scripps Research Institute's open data aggregation platform, integrating GISAID, WHO, and Johns Hopkins data streams.

4. Dimension Analysis

Cross-cutting analysis of the 10 scoring dimensions reveals structural strengths and weaknesses across the entire platform ecosystem:

4.1 Strongest Dimension: Security & Compliance (82.5)

Security is the highest-scoring dimension globally, driven by strong encryption standards, access controls, and regulatory compliance across defense (L3) and genomic (L2) platforms. HIPAA, GDPR, and ISO 27799 compliance is well-established in the top tiers.

4.2 Weakest Dimension: Accessibility (73.9)

Accessibility remains the greatest challenge. Many platforms lack mobile compatibility, offline capability, multi-language support, and training resources — creating a significant barrier for deployment in low-resource settings where surveillance is most needed.

4.3 Notable Gaps

  • Community Support (75.0) — Many government and defense platforms operate with limited community engagement
  • Visualization (76.6) — Interactive dashboards and geographic mapping capabilities remain inconsistent
  • Interoperability (77.3) — HL7/FHIR adoption is fragmented; data silos persist between civilian and defense systems

5. Layer Analysis

Performance across the five capability layers is remarkably consistent (77.2–79.5 average), suggesting that capability gaps are more about distribution and access than absolute technical quality. L2 Genomic leads slightly (79.5), driven by strong open-source bioinformatics tools, while L4 Hardware trails (77.2) due to the small number of highly specialized platforms.

6. Key Findings

6.1 Genomic Sequencing Inequity

Approximately 80% of global genomic sequencing capacity is concentrated in Five Eyes and EU nations. Africa, Central Asia, and Pacific Islands have near-zero autonomous sequencing capability, creating massive surveillance blind spots during pandemic events.

6.2 AI/ML Integration Divide

Only ~15% of evaluated platforms integrate meaningful AI/ML capabilities. DARPA-funded programs (PREEMPT, P3) and commercial platforms (BlueDot, Airfinity) lead, while the majority of LMIC-deployed platforms lack any AI integration.

6.3 One Health Fragmentation

Despite WHO advocacy, One Health integration — bridging human, animal, and environmental surveillance — remains the weakest convergence area. Only a handful of platforms (WAHIS, EMPRES-i, Predict) operate across these domains.

6.4 MCM Manufacturing Concentration

Medical countermeasure manufacturing capacity is concentrated in <10 countries. The COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated the fragility of this model, yet platform coverage for distributed manufacturing intelligence remains minimal.

6.5 Cyber-Bio Convergence Risk

As biosurveillance platforms become increasingly digital, the cyber-bio attack surface is expanding. Only 12% of platforms demonstrate integrated cybersecurity postures. Genome databases and real-time surveillance feeds represent high-value targets.

10. Recommendations

10.1 Immediate Priorities (0–12 months)

  1. Establish genomic sequencing hubs in Africa, Central Asia, and Pacific Islands to close the surveillance blind spot.
  2. Mandate HL7/FHIR interoperability standards for all publicly funded surveillance platforms.
  3. Deploy AI/ML augmentation to existing LMIC surveillance systems, starting with DHIS2 and SORMAS deployments.
  4. Conduct cybersecurity audits of all platforms handling genomic data, particularly genome databases (GISAID, NCBI) and real-time surveillance feeds.

10.2 Medium-Term Goals (1–3 years)

  1. Create a unified Five Eyes biosurveillance data fabric bridging civilian and defense platforms.
  2. Fund distributed MCM manufacturing intelligence platforms to reduce single-source dependency.
  3. Develop One Health integration frameworks connecting human, animal, and environmental surveillance streams.
  4. Establish wastewater surveillance networks in all WHO regions as a cost-effective early warning system.

10.3 Long-Term Vision (3–10 years)

  1. Achieve global real-time pathogen surveillance coverage with <24h sample-to-sequence-to-alert capability.
  2. Build sovereign national genomic surveillance capacity in all WHO member states.
  3. Deploy autonomous AI-driven early warning systems capable of detecting novel pathogen emergence before clinical recognition.
